<Step id="w7-s1" title="Make the portfolio legible" minutes={35}>

No new code this week. The work is done; now you make it findable and legible to someone giving you ninety seconds. A profile README or an index repo that points to each project and states, one line each, what it proves.

The <DocLink href="/portfolio">portfolio page</DocLink> of this site is built for exactly this. Paste each week's link and it becomes a clean index you can send with an application, or copy into a README.

<Callout type="why">
Hiring managers skim. A tidy index with one honest sentence per project respects that, and respect gets read. A wall of repos with no context gets closed.
</Callout>

<Checkpoint id="w7-s1">Your projects are gathered in one place, each with a one line statement of what it proves.</Checkpoint>

</Step>

<Step id="w7-s2" title="Rewrite the CV and LinkedIn in quality language" minutes={45}>

Your past is an asset, once it is translated. Operations and management experience is full of quality work that was never labelled as such. Relabel it, honestly.

Catching problems before the customer does becomes detecting and mitigating risk. Coordinating people under pressure becomes stakeholder communication. Keeping a standard every single day becomes regression and consistency. None of this is invention. It is naming what you already did in the words the field uses.

<Callout type="tip">
Every rewritten line must still be true. "Detecting and mitigating risk" is fair for someone who caught problems early. Inflated claims fall apart in the first interview, which is the worst possible place for them to fall apart.
</Callout>

<Checkpoint id="w7-s2">Your CV and LinkedIn are in English and describe your past in quality terms, truthfully.</Checkpoint>

</Step>

<Step id="w7-s3" title="A thirty second story" minutes={20}>

Have a short answer ready for "so, tell me about yourself". Where you came from, what you built, where you are going. Thirty seconds, said plainly, no apology for the gap. A career break is a fact, not a confession.

<Callout type="why">
You will be asked some version of this in every conversation about the job. Deciding the answer once, in advance, means you are not composing it live while nervous. Preparation reads as confidence, because it more or less is confidence.
</Callout>

<Checkpoint id="w7-s3">You can tell your thirty second story out loud without reading it.</Checkpoint>

</Step>

<Step id="w7-s4" title="Apply" minutes={30}>

Send the application, with the portfolio attached. This is the point of the whole seven weeks, and it is the step people invent reasons to skip.

Waiting to feel ready is a losing strategy, because the feeling does not arrive on schedule and rarely arrives at all. You have the artifacts. You have the vocabulary. The green ticks are real. Apply while you are still slightly underprepared, like everyone who has ever got the job.

<Callout type="tip">
Point the first applications at roles like the <DocLink href="https://careers.umain.com/jobs/6665867-qa-engineer">one this course was built around</DocLink>. You now have direct evidence for most of what it asks. Evidence beats a confident tone every time.
</Callout>

<Checkpoint id="w7-s4">The application is sent, and the portfolio is live.</Checkpoint>

</Step>
